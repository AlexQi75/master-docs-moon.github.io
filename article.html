The Moon Shines Upon Us All

It’s never simply a group of people coming to see some prints created some centuries ago. We come all the way to see ourselves through the art.

By Alex Qi

A golden full moon shines over the dark winter woods and a flutist plays in the atmospheric glow. The bare and black boughs and branches stretch against the nighty sky, like the veins on the back of an old man’s hand. in the distance, two lanterns glitter from far behind the haze. Chill, sober, and contemplative.

“Toyama Plain,” a woodblock print created by Kawase Hasui in 1920, is one the pieces featured for an exhibit of traditional Japanese art. Hasui’s print gave the exhibit the poetic name – “By the Light of the Moon: Nocturnal Japanese Prints” at The Art Institute of Chicag o.

AIC is packed with tourists and art enthusiasts all day long, yet this small exhibition enjoys a secluded mood with far fewer visitors than the impressionist paintings upstairs.

Since the beginning of human civilization, the moon shining brightly in the night sky has been the inspiration for artistic creations in all forms. Its powerful and tranquil presence gives viewers a moment to pause, relax or contemplate spiritual or philosophical meaning.

“Moon is the symbol of wisdom,” said Jana Vander Lee, a visitor from Houston, an art writer and a weaver. Her favorite work in the show is a print that depicts the moon shining over the shimmering sea. The light glazes a silver lining on the distant cloud, the edges of branches and the needles on the pine tree by the shore, under which a lone fisherman, pole on his shoulder is cast in the dark shadow. We can’t see his face, but we can feel the mood.

This artwork, entitled “Spring Moon, Ninomiya Beach,” was also created by Hasui (1883–1957), an artist seemingly obsessed with depicting the evening’s aura. “

Asian arts tend to depict nature with a sense of infinity, while western arts are more likely to emphasize the changing force of nature,” Lee commented. “This one, with its excellent workmanship and delicacy, gives a sense of solitude and communing with nature. It’s very meditative.”

“I appreciate the oriental touch, soft and subtle,” said Nathaniel Yang, an undergraduate student studying math and science at Northwestern University. A member of the Art Institute, he frequents the museum now and then. “What’s also interesting to me is to see how the wood print has evolved through time.”

In the earliest prints featured in this exhibition, the darkness of the night is often rendered as a solid wall of black placed behind figures who are lit only by the glow of lanterns or hovering fireflies. By the 19th century, the evening itself becomes the focus, inspiring a more philosophical approach. This refinement of nocturnal perception is developed further in the 20th century, as artists like Hasui explored the complexity of night shadows and the moon’s reflection on water.

Besides the artistic approach to appreciate the art, some visitors came to savor the mood of home.

“This place is near where we used to live,” Atsumi Murao told me, pointing to “Tenno Temple, Osaka,” another piece by Hasui. It depicts temple buildings with red walls and black tiles, bathed in the snow that falls lightly from the sky, mingling with what might be stars. Again, there is a lonely figure outside the closed door, standing back to us under a snow-covered umbrella, with a string of foot prints behind.

“But I have never seen this place,” Murao said. She moved from Japan to Chicago two years ago.

For Jamie Frazier, the feeling is more nostalgic, vague but intense.

“This is also part of my cultural heritage,” said Frazier, who was visiting from Seattle with his wife. Frazier’s mother, growing up in Hakodate, Hokkaido, Japan’s northernmost island, met his father who was an American soldier deployed at Misawa Airforce Base after the World War II. They got married and moved to Washington. However, the cultural barrier, the racism in America in the 1960s, and a “family tragedy” all added up to a traumatic break up.

“I was six years old when she left in the 1970s. She survived 20 years after that in Japan. But I never got to see her again,” Frazier said. He made his first visit to his mother’s hometown two years ago and there he met his uncle.

Now he’s working on a book that will collect memories from the broken pieces as a result of the clashes between the two cultures.

It’s never simply a group of people coming to see some prints created some centuries ago. We come all the way to see ourselves through the art. Lee saw the pursuit of wisdom, while Yang witnessed the change in time. Frazier searched for his lost cultural root, and Murao recollected the memory of her hometown. We are part of what we see in a show and we eternalize art by adding mortal meanings to it.

That's also why it is typical in oriental art that the mood of a character is seldom depicted via facial expressions and body movements. Man is put in nature who speaks for us with its soaring peaks and surging rivers, with its summer flowers and snowy winters. We are part of the universe; and at the same time, we are the universe.

I stepped back, looking at the glass frames. They were like windows on the wall of time. We peeked into the life scenes of some Japanese men under the moonlight one or two centuries ago, while they, from the other side, caught sight of us, who came from different corners of the world, driven by different dreams and longings, and at last gathered by the light of the moon. It shines upon us all.
